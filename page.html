Gerrymandering

Why should we care about gerrymandering?

Every two years, the entire House of Representatives is re-elected. This body of 435 legislators is intended, as the name suggests, to be representative of the American electorate. In 2010, this meant about 700,00 people per representative . Given this seemingly straightforward relationship, one would expect, on average, that the political makeup of the House of Representatives would reflect the political makeup of the US voting population.

Of course, the relationship isn’t so simple. Representatives in actuality are elected from States, which are then divided into Congressional districts. The determination of the geography of these districts is where gerrymandering enters the picture. Gerrymandering is the process of “dividing of a state [] into election districts so as to give one political party a majority in many districts while concentrating the voting strength of the other party into as few districts as possible".

What is the best strategy for gerrymandering?

Gerrymandering is to some degree in the interests of both parties, regardless of whether they’re in power. The redrawing of districts is generally determined by whichever party is the majority in the State legislature or holds the Governorship , and therefore is a highly politicized process. The interests in this process differ for the party as a whole versus an individual representative:

  • For the parties, the goal is to maximize the number of seats they hold and minimize the seats for the opposition party. This means that the optimal strategy is to place as many of the opposing party’s voters in as few districts as possible, resulting in few districts that are won by a large margin. Then, they would like to create many districts won by their own party, but by a lesser (but still comfortable) margin.

  • For the individual representatives, we can cynically assume their only interest is to stay elected in their district. Therefore, they would be glad to be placed in a district with a very high probability of their party’s win. Oddly enough, this means that a Congressperson from one party could encourage the redrawing of their district in a way that the opposing party that is in control would also like.

Do we see gerrymandering happening in the data?

Gerrymandering benefits different parties in different states

The Republican Party benefits from gerrymandering in more states than does the Democratic Party.

  • 27 states were gerrymandered to benefit the Republican Party.
  • 9 states were not influenced at all by gerrymandering, 7 of which only have one representative, making gerrymandering impossible.
  • 14 states are gerrymandered to benefit the Democratic Party.

While the Democratic Party has the two highest states with the most 'gerrymandered representatives', the Republican Party has more overall 'gerrymandered representatives'.

'Gerrymandered representatives' are calculated by multiplying the percent of the state's House delegation that is in the 'wrong' party according to the state-wide vote by the total number of delegates in that state.

While California and New York have the two highest values and favor the Democratic Party, the Republican Party has more 'gerrymandered representatives' in total: 22.21 for the Republican Party and 17.63 for the Democratic Party.

Also, there is some pretty good evidence that the party controlling the state government strongly influences in which direction the gerrymandering occurs.

Why is the Republican Party better at gerrymandering?

Looking back at the discussion of strategy surrounding gerrymandering, a successful gerrymanderer will look to win elections for their party with a moderate but comfortable margin, and will look to force the other party into fewer victories with higher margins.

The Republican Party succeeds in following the strategy of winning with moderate margins, and losing to large margins:

Republican wins generally have margins of 15 - 40 percentage points. Democratic wins, on the other hand, have a much wider range and extend into the range of 60 - 90 percentage point wins. These results favor the Republican party, as it effectively means that fewer voters are required in order to gain the same number of seats.

Are these results strategic or structural?

In all likelihood, it is a combination of both. Democratic voters are very heavily concentrated in urban areas, while Republican voters are a bit more spread out. This means that elections in urban areas will often results in large margin wins for Democrats, regardless of the extent of gerrymandering. That being said, however, there are a good number of congressional districts whose shape is headscratch-worthy.
